# Legal Fair-Use Posture — `@baddesiplots`

> Defensible posture, not legal advice. We are not lawyers. Consult counsel before responding to any actual legal threat. This 1-pager exists so we can move fast in week one without flinching at the first email that mentions "infringement."

---

## What we do (the factual frame)

Bad Desi Plots posts short, intentionally bad **summaries** of named, copyrighted Bollywood and Tollywood films (e.g. *DDLJ*, *RRR*, *Baahubali*, *Sholay*). We name the films. We never reproduce dialogue verbatim, song lyrics, screenplay text, screen captures, posters, promotional stills, or trailer footage. The output is original prose written by humans (or LLMs under human review) that comments on, parodies, and exaggerates the plot to comic effect.

The product is **commentary and parody about films**. It is not the films, not derived art using the films' protected expression, and not a substitute for watching the films.

---

## US fair-use four-factor analysis (17 U.S.C. §107)

1. **Purpose and character of the use.** Transformative parody and criticism. We take the underlying *idea* of a movie (which is not copyrightable — see *Baker v. Selden*) and produce new expressive commentary that comments on the absurdity of common Bollywood tropes. *Campbell v. Acuff-Rose* (1994) established that parody of a famous work is presumptively transformative even when commercial. Our use is currently non-commercial (no paid product, no ads); commerciality may grow but transformativeness is the dominant factor.
2. **Nature of the copyrighted work.** The films are creative and published. This factor weighs slightly against us, but it carries the least weight in modern fair-use analysis (*Authors Guild v. Google*, 2015).
3. **Amount and substantiality used.** We use **the title and a paraphrased plot premise**. Titles are not copyrightable (*Arthur Rutenberg Homes v. Drew Homes*). Plot premises at the idea level are not copyrightable (idea-expression dichotomy). We do not reproduce protected expression — no dialogue, no lyrics, no images, no extended summaries that track scene-by-scene structure.
4. **Effect on the market.** Zero. A 30-word terrible plot summary is not a market substitute for a 3-hour theatrical film. If anything, the cards drive viewing (parody as advertising — *SunTrust Bank v. Houghton Mifflin*).

**Net:** factors 1, 3, and 4 favor us strongly; factor 2 is a wash. This is a defensible US fair-use posture.

## Indian fair-dealing analysis (Copyright Act, 1957)

§52(1)(a)(ii) — fair dealing for purposes of "criticism or review, whether of that work or of any other work" is not infringement. Indian courts (*Civic Chandran v. Ammini Amma*, Kerala HC 1996; *India TV Independent News v. Yashraj Films*, Delhi HC 2012) apply a four-part test substantially similar to US fair use: quantum and value of work taken, purpose, likelihood of competition, and whether use is genuinely critical/transformative.

Our cards are explicitly criticism (loving, parodic criticism) of named films. Quantum taken is minimal (premise + title). No competition. Purpose is critical/comedic commentary. India's fair-dealing standard is narrower than US fair use, but our use sits squarely inside §52(1)(a)(ii)'s carve-out for criticism and review.

## Trademark note

Film titles can be unregistered trademarks under Indian common law. We use titles **descriptively** ("this card is about *DDLJ*") not as a source identifier for our own product. Nominative fair use (*New Kids on the Block v. News America*, 9th Cir. 1992) protects this in the US; descriptive use is similarly protected in India.

We do not use studio logos, marks, or character names in branding. Account name (`@baddesiplots`) is original.

## What would change the analysis

Each of these would weaken our posture and trigger an internal review:

- Reproducing dialogue verbatim ("Mere paas Maa hai") — drop or paraphrase
- Using screenshots, posters, or stills
- Quoting lyrics
- Adopting a film's logo, font, or marks in branding
- Selling the cards (e.g. a paid deck) without re-reviewing
- Making the cards function as a substitute for watching the film (e.g. spoiler-heavy summaries)

If we ever cross any of these lines, escalate to counsel before posting.

## Takedown response playbook

When a takedown notice, cease-and-desist, or DMCA arrives:

### First 60 minutes
1. **Don't reply yet.** Don't post a reactive defense on the brand account. Silence is fine.
2. **Capture everything** — screenshot the email/DM, preserve headers, save the URL of the disputed post, and log to `incidents/` folder with a timestamp.
3. **Identify the sender's authority.** Is this an actual rights-holder (Yash Raj, Dharma, Arka Media, T-Series, etc.), a law firm representing them, or an opportunistic random? Three different responses.

### Decision tree

| Sender | First move |
|---|---|
| Major studio legal (YRF, Dharma, T-Series, Arka, AA Films, etc.) or their outside counsel | Pull the specific card within 24h. Reply politely (template below). Do not argue fair use in writing without counsel. Document. |
| Independent producer / smaller rights-holder | Pull the card. Reply politely. Offer to credit them or feature their other films positively. Many small producers want goodwill more than a legal win. |
| Random "I'm a fan and this isn't allowed" | Ignore unless it escalates. No legal force. |
| Instagram automated DMCA takedown | Comply with IG's process. File counter-notice ONLY if the takedown is clearly bogus AND we've consulted counsel. Counter-notices waive jurisdictional defenses. |
| Court-issued injunction or notice from Indian court | Stop everything. Pull all related cards. Get counsel today. |

### Template: polite response to a cease-and-desist

```
Subject: Re: [Their Reference Number / Subject Line]

Dear [Name],

Thank you for your email regarding the Instagram post(s) at [URL].

We have removed the post(s) in question. We are a small independent
project that creates original parodic commentary about films we love.
We have no intention of infringing on [Studio Name]'s rights, and we
appreciate you flagging this directly rather than escalating.

If there are specific guidelines from [Studio Name] about how fan
commentary may reference your titles, we would welcome them and will
adjust our approach accordingly.

Best regards,
The Bad Desi Plots team
hello@baddesiplots.com
```

Tone: cooperative, confident, not apologetic about the underlying activity. We did not do anything wrong. We are choosing to take it down because it is cheaper than litigation, not because we are conceding the point.

### Don't do these
- **Don't argue fair use in writing without counsel.** Anything you write becomes evidence. "We removed the post" is the safest sentence.
- **Don't admit to anything that isn't true.** Don't say "we won't post about your films again" — that's a contract you can't keep.
- **Don't go viral about it.** Subtweeting a studio for sending a takedown wins one news cycle and loses 10 years of relationship.
- **Don't ignore it.** Silence escalates. Reply within 5 business days.

## Risk inventory

- **Most likely scenario:** an automated IG DMCA filter takes down a post with a misidentified copyright claim. Counter-notice via IG's flow, document the false positive.
- **Second most likely:** a junior associate at a studio sends a templated C&D about a card that's been doing well. Pull the card, polite reply, move on.
- **Low likelihood:** actual lawsuit. Requires us to have a meaningful audience, refuse to take down content, and have something worth suing for. Not a week-1 concern.

We log every takedown in `docs/legal-incidents.md` (create on first incident). If we receive 3+ takedowns from the same studio, escalate to counsel and reconsider featuring that studio's catalog at all.

---

*This posture is defensible, not bulletproof. Real legal threats get real lawyers. This document exists so we don't panic-shut-down the brand the first time someone sends a stern email.*
